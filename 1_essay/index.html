On Not Being Afraid of a Blank Slate
Creative director Piera Gelardi discusses her role at Refinery29, what can be learned from past failures, and always prioritizing your own creativity. I can relate since all the content used in the book is created by me, and starting fresh on a blank slate can be the most difficult part of design - just getting it started.

We are focused on providing tools, not rules, to our audience so we try to create content that is a resource, that has a take-away, that doesn’t dictate how someone should live their life, but instead gives them information and resources to make their own decisions.

There’s so much judgment around taste and around change and around things that are considered “millennial” in nature.

Something about creative people that were launching their own weird, wacky, inspiring things.

I am definitely very entrepreneurial myself, so I love a blank slate, I love creating something new. I like the challenge of that and I get bored doing the same thing over and over again.

I tend to lean towards perfectionism and I have a high standard of excellence for myself and others, so a deadline helped me to learn when that’s useful and when that’s not useful.

When you have a high bar for excellence, you’re probably never going to put out something that’s just totally shitty. Even if I’m putting something out that I feel like is at 60%, that’s probably someone else’s 125% or something.

Staying connected to the creative people here is also really important to me. I need to feel creative. I need to feel fulfilled in that way.

I wanted to be able to play to my strengths. I still wore every possible hat when we were launching Refinery, but I also had these talented partners around me to fill in any gaps.

I prioritize my own creative inspiration as often as possible.

At the same time, you want to have the flexibility to allow the talented people that we’ve hired to express their own voices within our larger voice. I think a lot about how we can educate, empower, and inspire people to create within our brand.

I studied art, so I love art and design. I’m fascinated with expressing creativity very vibrantly and colourfully in a very multi-faceted dimension.

When you have a high bar for excellence, you’re probably never going to put out something that’s just totally shitty. Even if I’m putting something out that I feel like is at 60%, that’s probably someone else’s 125% or something.

We were really excited about the idea and it was a fun, creative thing, but ultimately it didn’t work out because we all had basically the exact same skill set.

I told everyone that if I see something that I think is really off, I’m going to let you know. It’s not to make you stop what you’re doing and scramble and fix it, it’s just for your reference moving forward or to start a dialogue so I can also understand how you were thinking.

When I started Refinery, I was suddenly creative in a different way. I was much more entrepreneurial and, between the four co-founders, I was someone that knew how to take an idea and make it into a reality.
